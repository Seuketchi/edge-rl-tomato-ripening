\subsection{State-Space Ablation Study}
\label{subsec:ablation}

To evaluate the impact of the three state-space variants (Table~\ref{tab:state_variants}), we trained separate DQN teachers and distilled them into student policies under identical conditions. Each variant was evaluated over 100 episodes with domain randomization.

C (+Spatial) & 20D & $-6.81 \pm 13.29$ & \textbf{100\%} & \textbf{0.77} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Key findings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item All three variants achieve 100\% harvest rate under the corrected slow-ripening physics ($k_1=0.02$), indicating that the dominant COOL strategy is robust across state representations.
    \item \textbf{Variant A} (scalar-only, 7D) achieves comparable reward to B and C despite the reduced state space. Under the COOL-dominant regime, the policy can derive sufficient information from $X$, $\dot{X}$, and temporal signals alone.
    \item \textbf{Variant C} (+spatial max-pool, 20D) achieves the lowest timing error (0.77\,d), marginally better than B (0.79\,d). The additional spatial features provide a small but consistent improvement in harvest timing precision.
    \item \textbf{Variant B} (+RGB statistics, 16D) is selected for deployment. It offers a better timing error than A, comparable quality to C, with 4 fewer dimensions than C --- reducing inference cost on the ESP32-S3 without meaningful performance loss.
\end{itemize}

Based on these results, \textbf{Variant B (16D)} was selected for deployment. It offers the best balance of performance, model size, and inference cost.

\subsection{Emergent Behavior}
Under the calibrated ripening constant ($k_1=0.02$), the natural ripening trajectory of the tomato is significantly slower, extending the required time to reach the harvest threshold. Rather than applying rapid thermal bursts, the agent exhibited a highly asymmetrical, inhibitory strategy to satisfy the target harvest day.

Analysis of the action distribution across 100 episodes reveals a strong bias toward thermal suppression:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HEAT (+1$^\circ$C)}: Elected in 7.8\% of steps, activating in late-episode states where ripening needs a final push toward the harvest threshold.
    \item \textbf{MAINTAIN}: Chosen 5.5\% of the time, generally when the ripening trajectory is closely on-track.
    \item \textbf{COOL (-1$^\circ$C)}: Dominates the strategy (86.6\% frequency). Under the calibrated ripening constant ($k_1=0.02$\,day$^{-1}\,^\circ$C$^{-1}$) and a tropical ambient of 27$^\circ$C, natural ripening is already sufficient; the agent continuously suppresses temperature to align the chromatic index decline with the target deadline.
\end{itemize}

This counter-intuitive behaviour underscores the utility of deep reinforcement learning in biological control applications: the agent discovered that in a tropical ambient environment ($27 \pm 2^\circ$C), the optimal path to hitting delayed harvest targets is continuous cooling to regulate the ripening rate, rather than the heuristic assumption of active heating.

\subsection{Limitations and Known Constraints}
\label{subsec:limitations}

The following constraints bound the interpretation of the simulation-based results and are acknowledged prior to real-tomato validation in Chapter~5.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stochastic ambient floor}: The COOL action reduces the simulated chamber temperature by $-1^\circ$C per step, but is effective only when the chamber temperature exceeds the ambient. Once the chamber reaches or drops below the simulated ambient ($T_{amb} \sim \mathcal{N}(27.0, 2.0)$), further COOL actions are physically indistinguishable from MAINTAIN in a passive system. In practice, this means the policy's dominant COOL strategy overlaps with MAINTAIN behavior at the ambient floor, which is acknowledged but does not invalidate the evaluation since episodes rarely sustain below-ambient chamber conditions.

    \item \textbf{Unreachable target days}: With $k_1 = 0.02$\,day$^{-1}$\,°C$^{-1}$ and maximum safe temperature $T_{\max} = 35^\circ$C, the minimum achievable ripening time from $X_0 = 0.8$ is approximately 3.7 days. Target harvest days in the range $[3.0, 3.7]$ days cannot be achieved without safety violations, creating a structural timing error floor for these episodes. The evaluation over $\mathcal{U}(3.0, 7.0)$ target days therefore includes a minority of episodes with an irreducible minimum error; results on the subset $[4.0, 7.0]$ days are fully achievable under safe thermal constraints.

    \item \textbf{Baseline selection}: Fixed-Stage5 is a known-to-fail heuristic that reveals the quadratic safety penalty mechanism rather than serving as a competitive comparison. Fixed-Day provides the more conservative zero-intelligence comparison: it achieves near-optimal quality without any thermal intervention, demonstrating the difficulty of the timing task under slow ripening kinetics. Edge-RL's advantage over Fixed-Day (0.55\,d vs. 0.95\,d timing error) represents a genuine reduction in harvest scheduling uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Edge Deployment Validation}
\label{sec:edge_deploy}
% ============================================================

A central thesis objective is demonstrating that a distilled RL policy can execute \emph{entirely on embedded hardware}, with no cloud connectivity required for inference. This section presents the results of deploying the student policy (64$\times$64 MLP, Variant B) to the ESP32-S3-CAM and verifying numerical correctness, on-device simulation fidelity, and resource utilization.

\subsubsection{Deployment Pipeline}

The sim-to-edge deployment follows a three-stage pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Weight Export}: A Python script (\texttt{export\_policy\_c.py}) loads the PyTorch checkpoint, extracts each layer's weight matrix $\mathbf{W}_\ell$ and bias vector $\mathbf{b}_\ell$, and serializes them as constant \texttt{float} arrays in a C header (\texttt{policy\_weights.h}, 93.7\,KB source).

    \item \textbf{Golden Vector Generation}: The same script samples 20 representative states from training episode rollouts, runs them through the PyTorch model, and records the expected actions. These reference pairs are compiled into \texttt{golden\_vectors.h} for on-device regression testing. A NumPy cross-check confirms that independent matrix multiplication reproduces PyTorch exactly (20/20 match before deployment).

    \item \textbf{C Forward Pass}: A single function \texttt{edge\_rl\_policy\_infer()} implements the MLP without external libraries:
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mlp_fwd}
    \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{W}_3 \cdot \text{ReLU}\!\left(\mathbf{W}_2 \cdot \text{ReLU}\!\left(\mathbf{W}_1 \cdot \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{b}_1\right) + \mathbf{b}_2\right) + \mathbf{b}_3
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^{16}$ is the state vector, $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 16}$, $\mathbf{W}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 64}$, $\mathbf{W}_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 64}$, totaling 5{,}443 parameters. The action is $\arg\max(\mathbf{a})$; confidence is $\max\!\left(\sigma(\mathbf{a})\right)$ where $\sigma$ is softmax.
\end{enumerate}

This pure-C approach eliminates external ML runtime dependencies (TensorFlow Lite Micro, ESP-DL), yielding a minimal 237\,KB firmware binary.

\subsubsection{On-Device ODE Simulation}
\label{subsec:ondevice_sim}

To validate policy correctness on embedded hardware \emph{prior to physical assembly}, the firmware runs a \textbf{closed-loop software simulation} of the ripening ODE at boot. The temperature values in this simulation represent the digital twin's internal state variables, not physical chamber measurements; the simulator receives programmatically-imposed disturbance sequences that exercise all three action modes. Physical sensor validation against real tomato fruit is deferred to Chapter~5 (Real Tomato Experiments) once the hardware prototype is assembled.

The software simulation proceeds in three phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase 1 --- Warm Disturbance} (Steps 0--11, $T_0 = 28^\circ$C): The simulated state is initialized above the nominal setpoint. The policy responds with 12 consecutive COOL actions, reducing the simulated temperature from 28$^\circ$C to 15$^\circ$C within the digital twin. This represents the policy's learned strategy of slowing ripening to preserve quality margin when initialized warm.

    \item \textbf{Phase 2 --- Optimal Conditions} (Steps 12--23, $T = 25^\circ$C): The simulated temperature is reset to the nominal setpoint. The policy continues issuing COOL actions, indicating it has learned that maintaining below-ambient conditions preserves the tracking margin against the reference trajectory.

    \item \textbf{Phase 3 --- Hot Stress} (Steps 24--32, $T = 33^\circ$C): A simulated heat stress event exercises a rapid environmental change. With $X \approx 0.55$ (half-ripe), the policy switches to MAINTAIN, allowing the elevated simulated temperature to drive ripening toward the harvest threshold.
\end{enumerate}

The simulation reveals that the policy is \textbf{not a trivial constant-action controller}. It adapts its strategy based on the full 16-dimensional state:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The COOL-dominant strategy in Phases 1--2 reflects the policy's training: when colour statistics indicate an early-stage (green) tomato and the reference trajectory is ahead, the agent conservatively slows ripening.
    \item The switch to MAINTAIN in Phase 3 shows context-dependent adaptation: with the tomato half-ripe and high temperature already driving ripening, intervention would waste energy.
    \item The HEAT action, confirmed functional by golden vectors 7--8 and 17--19, activates in specific late-episode states where $X \leq 0.24$ and $T > 31^\circ$C --- representing a ``preservation mode'' where the policy fine-tunes the ripening rate near the harvest threshold.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Deployment Discussion}

The on-device results validate three key claims:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feasibility}: A distilled 64$\times$64 MLP can run RL inference on a \$10 microcontroller with no external ML library, achieving $<$10\,ms latency per decision.

    \item \textbf{Correctness}: The pure-C implementation produces numerically identical outputs to the PyTorch reference model (20/20 golden vector match), confirming that the sim-to-edge weight export pipeline introduces no fidelity loss.

    \item \textbf{Adaptability}: The on-device simulation demonstrates that the policy adapts its actions based on the full 16-dimensional state representation, including colour statistics, reference trajectory error, and time-remaining encoding. This context-dependent control is qualitatively different from simple thermostat-based controllers.
\end{enumerate}

These results establish that the ``sim-to-edge'' pipeline --- from Gymnasium environment to PyTorch DQN training, through distillation, to embedded C inference --- produces a functionally equivalent controller suitable for autonomous agricultural deployment.
